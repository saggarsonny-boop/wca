## Article 1: Medical / Clinical Focus
- **Target Outlets:** KevinMD, MedPage Today, Doximity
- **Title:** The Invisible Sentence: How HHS-OIG Exclusions and Licensing Actions Outlast Criminal Sentences
- **Author:** Sonny Saggar, MD

For licensed healthcare professionals, the resolution of a federal criminal case is rarely the end of the machinery. While a defendant's attention is naturally consumed by sentencing guidelines, presentence reports, and potential custody terms, the administrative "stack" that follows is often what terminates their career.

As an emergency physician trained at Oxford and Barts who navigated the federal system firsthand, I observed a critical systemic gap: defendants and their defense counsels are often blind to the collateral exclusions that follow a conviction.

### The HHS-OIG Exclusion Stack
Under 42 U.S.C. § 1320a-7, the Department of Health and Human Services (HHS) Office of Inspector General (OIG) imposes mandatory exclusions on individuals convicted of program-related crimes, patient abuse, or felony healthcare fraud. 
1. **Mandatory vs. Permissive Exclusions:** Mandatory exclusions last a minimum of 5 years. Permissive exclusions can be triggered by license suspensions or misdemeanor convictions.
2. **The Excluded Provider Database (LEIE):** Being placed on the LEIE means no federal healthcare program (Medicare, Medicaid) can pay for items or services provided by the excluded individual.

### Rebuilding a Professional Life
Navigating professional restoration requires understanding that licensing boards and OIG act independently of the U.S. District Court. Preparation for reentry must begin during the presentence phase. White Collar Academy (https://whitecollaracademy.com) has mapped these administrative hurdles to help physicians plan for the professional stack they will face post-supervision.

## Article 2: Legal / Practice Focus
- **Target Outlets:** Law360, Law.com, ABA Journal
- **Title:** Structuring the Sentencing Mitigation Workflow: Eliminating Attorney-Client Friction
- **Author:** Sonny Saggar, MD

In federal criminal defense, the Presentence Investigation (PSI) and the resulting Presentence Report (PSR) dictate almost every aspect of a client's custody and program eligibility. Yet, attorneys frequently encounter client friction during this phase—either from clients who shut down due to trauma, or clients who submit unstructured, irrelevant narratives.

To optimize mitigation outcomes and secure departures or variances under 18 U.S.C. § 3553(a), law firms must structure the client sentencing mitigation workflow.

### The Intake Bottleneck
Traditional intake relies on unstructured client interviews, creating paralegal overhead. By asking clients to complete structured, self-guided workbooks during the pre-plea phase, defense teams gain two advantages:
- **Client Agency:** The client compiles dates, medical logs, and character reference templates independently, reducing anxiety.
- **Data Clarity:** The attorney receives a structured Case File that maps cleanly to the PSR sections.

## Article 3: General Focus
- **Target Outlets:** Medium, Substack, LinkedIn
- **Title:** From Oxford ER to Federal Prison Camp: What I Found on the Road
- **Author:** Sonny Saggar, MD

I spent twenty-five years as an emergency physician. My training at Oxford and Barts in London taught me to read complex physiological systems under pressure, triage trauma, and explain crisis points plainly to frightened families. 

But nothing prepared me for the trauma of entering the federal criminal justice system as a defendant.

When you are indicted, your world collapses in a matter of hours. The machinery of the U.S. government is massive, and you are immediately overwhelmed. I wrote *The Road* (https://whitecollaracademy.com/the-road) because I realized that the hardest part of the process isn't the physical confinement—it is the mental isolation of walking through the machinery blind.

### The Power of a Map
In the ER, we use clinical pathways. In the federal justice system, you need a visual map. You must understand where the forks in the road lie:
- Investigation to Indictment
- Plea Negotiation vs. Trial
- The PSR interview and custody point calculation
- Supervised Release and Reentry

If you or a loved one is navigating this process, remember: you are facing a system, not a personal judge. Map the system, understand the points, and focus on the life that comes after.
